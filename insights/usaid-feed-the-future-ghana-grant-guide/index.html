USAID Feed the Future Ghana: Current Status and Where to Look Instead

USAID's Feed the Future programme in Ghana was suspended in 2025 and has not been reinstated. Here is what actually happened, and the active funding routes Ghanaian agribusinesses should pursue in 2026.

AgriValue Advisory ConsultUpdated July 20266 min read
Important update — July 2026

This article previously described the USAID Feed the Future Market Systems and Resilience (MSR) Activity as an active grant window. It is not. USAID funding to Ghana, including MSR, was suspended in January 2025 and has not been restored. We have rewritten this article to reflect the current situation accurately and to point you toward funding routes that remain genuinely active.

What happened to USAID Feed the Future in Ghana?

In January 2025, the United States government abruptly suspended USAID funding to Ghana and dozens of other countries. This was part of a much larger action — Congress was informed that 5,341 USAID programmes were terminated, roughly 86% of the agency's entire former portfolio, with only a small number of programmes classified as "life-saving" retained.

Feed the Future did not fall into the protected category. The Feed the Future Ghana Market Systems and Resilience (MSR) Activity — a five-year, roughly $35.9–37.8 million programme implemented by ACDI/VOCA to support smallholder farmers and agribusinesses across Northern Ghana — lost its funding pipeline along with the rest of USAID's Ghana portfolio. The related Ghana Trade and Investment Activity was affected in the same way.

The research side of Feed the Future was hit just as hard. All but one of the 17 Feed the Future Innovation Labs — university-run research centres that had backed agricultural work in Ghana and other countries — were officially cancelled. Field research already underway in Ghana, including trials on indigenous vegetables, was halted overnight when termination notices went out in February 2025.

Is there any sign of it coming back?

Not as of the most recent reporting. As of February 2026, researchers and implementing partners who lost USAID funding have largely moved on to build independent successor initiatives rather than wait for the programme to be restored. There is no indication from USAID or the Ghanaian government that MSR or the broader Feed the Future portfolio will be reinstated.

President Mahama noted in mid-2026 that Ghana lost an estimated $78 million from the closure of USAID programmes across sectors, with humanitarian assistance globally down by roughly 40%.

Practical implication for agribusinesses

If you run an agribusiness, cooperative, or NGO in Ghana and are looking for grant funding, USAID and Feed the Future are not currently a viable route. Do not build a funding strategy around them. The funders below remain genuinely active and are where we now direct our Funding-Ready Pack clients.

Where Ghanaian agribusinesses should look instead

1. IFAD / MOFA — GASIP Matching Grants

The Ghana Agriculture Sector Investment Programme (GASIP), funded by IFAD and channelled through the Ministry of Food and Agriculture (MOFA), continues to operate. It offers sub-grants to farmer-based organisations, cooperatives, and agribusiness SMEs, with a strong focus on smallholder value chain development in Northern Ghana. Co-financing is typically required, and women must represent a minimum share of direct beneficiaries.

2. AfDB — Agriculture Fast Track (AFT) Fund

The African Development Bank's AFT Fund provides grants of $50,000 to $1.5 million to prepare larger agribusiness investments — feasibility studies, environmental assessments, and financial modelling — for projects valued at $1 million or more. Ghana has a track record with this fund, and it remains open to new applications through the AfDB Ghana Country Office.

3. GIZ — Agribusiness Facility for Africa (ABF)

GIZ's ABF Matching Grant Fund supports private sector-led agribusiness projects across ECOWAS countries including Ghana, with a particular focus on cashew, maize, and livestock value chains. It requires co-financing from the applicant but has a shorter, more predictable call cycle than the DFI alternatives.

"The funding landscape has genuinely shifted. Agribusinesses that built plans around USAID need to redirect that effort — the opportunity has not disappeared, it has moved to IFAD, AfDB, and GIZ."

What this means if you were planning a USAID application

  • Do not submit an unsolicited USAID or Feed the Future application — there is no active window to receive it
  • Redirect any USAID-specific preparation work — much of the documentation (financial statements, organisational registration, beneficiary data) is reusable for IFAD, AfDB, or GIZ applications
  • Reassess your target value chain and geography against the funders above, since eligibility rules differ meaningfully between them
  • Watch for AfDB's proposed $500 million smallholder financing facility, announced in 2025 and still under consultation — this may open a significant new window once implemented
